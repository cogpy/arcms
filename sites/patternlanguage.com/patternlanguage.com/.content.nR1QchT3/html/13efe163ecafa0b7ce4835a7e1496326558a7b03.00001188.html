Often people describe being "receivers" of "creative information" in a very passive way when it actually "comes,"--dreams, trances, absorbtive reveries--even though they may have much more strenuously "set the stage" by making many kinds of preparations--consciously studying something, imitating masters, playing scales, etc. Edison comes to mind as someone who really busted ass on exploratory investigations as part of the process, whereas Edgar Cayce would just lie down and enter a sleep-like trance state. For almost thirty years now, I have been a student of Adi Da Samraj. He often will pose a question that acts as a koan (to use the Buddhist term) such as, "What do you do, exactly, when you want to have a thought?" to point out that many times we assume that we are the active agent in situations, but actually it's not really the case. We aren't the agent that is doing the breathing or the digesting, for instance, even though our language is set up in such a way that we beg the question. We say,"Oh, well that digestion is being handled by my parasympathetic nervous system--it's all kind of automatic but it's me you know--'I'm' the CEO here." When actually it is more accurate to say we are being lived. And, we don't know what a single thing is. We can collect all kinds of descriptive information ABOUT things, but we can't account at all for the Isness of anything, the Beingness. Even though Adi Da has said "All there is is is," we don't know what "is" is.

One use I am interested in finding out how to use a computer for has to do with studying/replicating patterns on moth wings. Around here we have, in particular, some "Oak Moths" who have very complex, very fine patterns on their wings that seem to help them blend in with Oak Tree bark. Some years back I was reading with a lamp in a camp during the active season of the moths ,so they kept landing on my reading materials. Now, the colors they are using are not very attention grabbing--lots of greys and browns--so I had never really apppreciated them before. HOWEVER, when I got a really intimate look at ten or twenty, I saw that each one was different, like snowflakes, and their patterns were mysteriously, utterly spectacular, even though and maybe even BECAUSE OF the fact that they were using such an apparently limited palette. To try to explain: boundaries and gradients were amazingly utilized to produce design lines that were often extremely subtle and yet extremely sharp at the same time. Let's say there were one million grey tones. Sometimes a pattern would be formed by grey #597,652 outlining a field of grey #597,653. To form such a design by human efforts would, I imagine, take years and years just to make all the marks, not to mention deciding where to put them. With computer technology I think one could get a handle on first photograghing them and then scanning them, then replicating them and studying them to understand their appeal better and then hopefully using them in skillful ways.
One of the fascinating aspects of these wings is that although the designs seem infinitely varied and "wild" they also seem completely right, calm, rested! They all seem perfectly related to the shapes of the wings, which are very elegant and poised shapes, and their (the wing shapes') bilaterally symmetrical effect acts as counterpose to the apparent chaos of the patterns inside the wing field. Chaos is not the right word, there is profound freedom and profound order evident at the same time.It's the symmetry/asymmetry riddle of the Japanese kimono again. That is something I would really like to get a better understanding of. If you can point me to presently available computer tools to bring to bear, I would love it.
The next thing about moth/butterfly wings is the color lesson. I love looking at the things that they pull off, totally wild and yet with perfect poise. CA worried about his color choices on the Upham House turning out to be "silly." However, interesting color was necessary to enliven the grey mass, and the amount of color was so small that any vividness was not in danger of being too much, imo. I would have gone for color delineating the boundary of the edge of the roof as well, but under-coloring is one of my compaints about houses generally in this neck of the woods.

Museo di Castelvecchio
" At Castelvecchio the builders of olden times had tackled the problem of the formal identity of a series of dwellings and their connection. The rooms were laid out in rows, beween two walls distant and distinct from each other. They were the two facades of the castle. The more ancient of the two walls is solid, almost without apertures. It opened towards the river, on the outside. The other,more recent one was less massive and opened out onto the garden. You see how the building retains its identity in time: it's a basic principle....I wanted to preserve the originality, the character of every room, but I didn't want to use the wooden beams of the earlier restoration. Since the rooms were square, I set a paired steel beam to support the point where the two reinforced concrete beams crossed, so indicating the main lines of the building's formal structure. Where they crossed the importance of that square was emphasized because the crossing of the two beams in the center implies the pillar which helps define the square.
This is the visual logic I wanted to use as a frame of reference. The way the beams were made also brings out the visual logic, but only in the details. I could have used the steel profiles already on the market, but it would have been an engineering solution without any visual emphasis. I thought it would be more interesting to take the profiles and make a composition out of them. The new joints reveal the structure of the element and the new functions." p67

"Scarpa called for a return to the "ancient " discipline of drawing as the generative force of invention, and with it the recovery of the poetic independence of design, with a "distictive personal character that would lend each work the moral authority that everyone in the world of art must strive for." Beginning with a famous lecture in 1963, he strenuously defended decoration, arguing--with the example of the microphone stem's tubular column before him--that forms remain 'un-finite,' and thus inexpresssive, without it. He believed that a syntax of color and ornament becomes inevitable in an architectural project from the moment the designer makes the first choice of a material, resist as one might.
Scarpa also advocated a return to the sensory delight that comes from what can be touched and felt in each material, to the intellectual excitment that derives from exploring and unveiling its structural characteristics,and to the logical pleasures of seeing how distinctive materials with different properties can be wed. He asked us to re-acknowledge the potential of materials to drive the generative process and produce a ruling idea for the larger work:"It is a curious facultythis, which enables us to sense that a certain dimensional factor, a thickness for instance, can become the determining characteristic of the physical value of things." Above all, he insisted that the building of a vocabulary of material and ornament was a critical step in capturing those senses---tactile, psychological, and mnemonic, that open a work of architecture to "maximum expression and meaning." p13

Dear B. & V.
Mulling over your foray into repair- renovate-remodel, I was making a tricky cut for some lintel trim clearance, getting some old siding out of the way, when something possibly helpful occured to me.
First, there is the almost medical, surgical aspect.(Don't cut too deep or far.)
Second encompasses the "can of worms syndrome" of finding soft wood, outdated utility infrastructure, minimal insulation, foundation issues, etc.
Third, How does this change Tie In structurally?, esthetically?, investmentwise?
Fourth, how are human activities and uses, privacy, views etc. affected in the old and new sections of the work?

How many times have you heard people say "It's way easier to build new.", or " In hindsight we should have started over from scratch."?

Successfully done, there's few things more satisfying than a complementary addition or renovation, but the above oft-heard phrases are a testimony to the difficulty of pulling it off easily in the modern scenario.

I think the primary reason it is harder to renovate or remodel is that the old work must be constantly read for "centers" and "wholeness". And, the old work, or it's outlying border elements, are often in your way. Even out at the edge of a new addition there are visual, auditory, and other physical and psychic connections to the antecedent structure that will be made, harmoniously or not.
New centers must be established, old centers must be preserved, supported, re-interpreted. It gets to be a bit of a puzzle compared to making the thing up out of whole cloth....

Catch you later, Dirk

A further addition to our (including Vasco) consideration about remodeling/demolition, which even though it started as a curiosity offers a chance to cite an interesting thing:
I was looking through a paperback full of quotes by Picasso about art to various people over many years. It was obvious that he didn't care about contradicting himself at all, that he enjoyed being a trickster with his verbal communications. One thing he said that particularly struck me was a pronouncement that "painting is destruction." I thought he must be pulling the leg of whoever he was talking to. About a year later I happened to see a film that was made showing Picasso painting. He painted on plexiglass for the process. Hundertwasser advocated "window rights"-- which he defined as any tenant's right to decorate the exterior wall around his or her window as far as the length of the tenant's arm. He also recommended filling some units in apartment buildings with 3 feet of humus and planting trees that grow out the windows. He said these "tree tenants"(his term) would pay their rent by purifying the atmosphere and absorbing sound. These images first appeared in his paintings and later he carried them out for real. He thought of himself as a doctor for architecture to counteract modernist influences, which he felt were anti-life. (He was also an appreciator of the liveliness of the architecture in "slums.")
