Rob
I agree about doing tiles around the windows, budget permitting of course. Making tiles for any application lets you solve the "dutchman problem." I am amazed that people just accept "dutchmen" in tile installations. They are so used to accepting that tiles will not fit evenly in installations without partial cut tiles that even in expensive installations they don't insist on custom tiles being made to blend everything together more smoothly.

It seems to me that windows are a really great opportunity to beautify buildings, sadly, nearly always a missed chance.
Hundertwasser advocated "window rights"-- which he defined as any tenant's right to decorate the exterior wall around his or her window as far as the length of the tenant's arm. He also recommended filling some units in apartment buildings with 3 feet of humus and planting trees that grow out the windows. He said these "tree tenants"(his term) would pay their rent by purifying the atmosphere and absorbing sound. These images first appeared in his paintings and later he carried them out for real. He thought of himself as a doctor for architecture to counteract modernist influences, which he felt were anti-life. (He was also an appreciator of the liveliness of the architecture in "slums.")

Rob Asumendi 17:05:57 4/03/2004
I imagine that the relative success of roughness depends on its intent. The way I understand it, roughness comes about a) as natural consequence of paying attention to the unique configuration of existing centers and b) intentionally to soften the "brutal" rigidity of designed form, e.g. skyscraper windows. From Mr. Alexander's discussions of the tile layout in NOO 1 & 2, it seems the wall itself was the center being strengthened by the tiles, not the windows. He mentions gradients as the driving transformation here, not roughness. It seems the windows are strengthened by the empty space around them, perhaps void comming into play. So the roughness of the tiling pattern here is a consequence of keeping these voids equal size for uniquely positioned windows. I think the photo on page 295 shows this decision in better light than the photo on 287, because at this scale you can see that the gray area creates a better boundary for the window. I guess one would have to see the wall without the tiles before judging whether the tiles actually damage the windows as centers. I can see how choosing to lay the tiles to the very edges of the windows would have resulted in a "wallpaper" look, weakening the windows AND the walls as centers. Perhaps an even better solution would have been some more elaborate tile work surrounding the windows to give them a thicker membrane/boundary, but who knows, that could have blown the budget.

As for the plum tree, funny how a little plum tree can become so famous :)

Beijing, one of the famous historic and cultural cities of China, is also one of the lasting architectural miracles in the history of the world's civilization.

Especially those narrow Hutongs(lanes) arouse many reveries, and it takes a lot of learning to explore into their developments.

Hutongs of Beijing are decidedly not only the historical and present viens and capillaries of the city, but also the site where families live, and the important arena of a perfection which was reached in single story, high density, low cost residential architecture.

The successful happiness of the owners and renters and their love of the individual houses and neighborhoods are the essential evidence that the "pattern complex" is a good one.

The highly sustainable character over the centuries and present day desireability ensures that they are even more valuable to us now as we attempt to find ways, within our own time and culture, to make houses and lanes that are alive again, and capable of satisfying people on deep emotional levels.

The courtyard houses of the old hutongs are more in tune with nature, thus illustrating the concept of people as a part of nature and proposing an ideal alternative in the center of a large bustling metropolis, able to apply in many diverse global situations.

Many, very many, aspects of the lives of people and buildings are universally true everywhere, in all ages.

"It is a kind of enjoyment to sit under the grape vine in sweltering summer."

"Roofs designed like surging waves, white with snow."

A saying goes among old Beijing residents that, in Beijing, 360 Hutongs are given a name each, while those without names are beyond calculation.

Monumental Chinese architecture was traditionally laid out according to cosmological principles that became known as feng shui- away of arranging objects to avoid or deflect the invasion of evil forces.
the Daoist Buddhist and Confucian temples you see in China today are a product of these principles.
Protected by rectangular wallslike a small city the temples main entrance faces south.
Inside the massive wooden doors is a series of pavillions and couryards, lined on both sides by residential and administrative halls. The inner complex can be compact or vast, consisting of hundreds of buildings or a small handful.
The temple architecture is virtually identical regardless of the religion practiced within. Variations in style are a product of region and historical period, not the dictates of faith.
The main support consists of interlocking wooden beams and enormous columns resting on raised platforms. The curving roof is paved in ceramic tiles. In southern China elaborate ornamentation and increasingly upturned eaves became the fashion. In the north temple architecture remained more restrained.

Vasko Drogriski 17:09:24 3/03/2004
Chris
Yes, only a little in Australia. Lots of information around about it but not much experience using it.
My limited experience of it is that its labour intensive, particularly the rendering part. The bales can go up quickly but you have to watch out for lateral stability, as well as detailing around doors and windows. There is also the issue of load bearing or infill... load bearing is difficult, bales need to have great consistency in compactness, moisture content, particular straw should be used etc...
The general consensus down here seems to be it is particularly good for:
Rural situations where the bales are availabe and dont have to be transported long distances
Good for extremely cold and extremely hot climates
The bales have infinte insulation and no thermal mass, therefore around here they are often used as external walls
Not good for:
Damp climates
Urban buildings as the bales take up too much space.
I think they require more specialisation and experience than is thought.
Since the introduction of straw bale down here, there have been other 'straw' based waste products also, such as straw board panels. These are used as an internal cladding, much easier and faster to use.
Very early on I talked about my experience of how bad 'patterns' are so persistent in our culture.
I have had some more recent experiences and was wondering what others do about them.
The particular pattern I am talking about is 'Backyards'.
Just recently I completed a house design with clients, fairly young, in which we used many of CA's patterns... starting off with the major ones of siting, locating gardens in the best, sunniest positions, working through circulation realms and main room positions etc... The product was a great design. We also used some patterns that we invented ourselves, such as 'south facing outdoors', which is shade.
One day, the clients came to me after talking to friends and family, showing off their design we had made, and were told by people that they had no 'backyard'. Ofcoarse, they did have a backyard, but the point is it was not in the usual place, opposite in direction to the front yard, or directly behind the home. It was instead along the side of the home, on the sunny side.
This realisation was so powerfull, that all of a sudden the clients forgot much of the previous process and now were fixated on the problem of 'not having their Backyard'. The pattern of the backyard, like many other patterns, is so consistently repeated and deeply ingrained and entrehched as a pattern, that it pulls extremely hard, away from what I understand this 'unfolding process' to be, or away from people to do naturally what they should. I realise that this is no revelation to anyone here. But I am interested to know if there are any others who have had similar experiences with 'bad patterns' and what these patterns are.
I don't mean to divert attention from the current trajectory of the discussions, but I find that these more mundane everyday problems do not leave me free enough to think about the next level of discussion.
