Released in 1989, it became a smash, selling over 7 million copies through various incarnations and sequels.

Next came The Sims.

Inspired by the architectural theorist Christopher Alexander, who sees buildings as means to enhance human interaction, Wright in 1993 started to work on a new game. In it, players would try to make virtual people happy by designing the perfect house for them.

Focus groups hated the game, Wright said, and managers at his company were leery. To keep their founder happy, they gave Wright a small team to work on the project and, according to Wright, prayed the game would vanish.

Fortunately, their prayers went unheeded. The Sims, released in February 2000, is now the best-selling PC game of all time. Millions of players have spent days on end furnishing homes, developing careers and building love lives for their digital dolls.

That's no surprise to his filmmaking partner, Mike Winter, who said, "Will has more knowledge of human behavior than anyone I've ever come across."

Wright's learned even more from fans' interactions with The Sims -- lessons he's now applying to his robotic creations: like not making the machines too life-like.

"One of the reasons The Sims seem so realistic is because everything in the game is somewhat abstract. You see icons of what a Sims is talking about, not the exact words," Wright said. "It invites the player to come in and imagine the details. To pour personality into these empty vessels. If we gave The Sims too much detail, the illusion would break."

Wright uses the example of R2D2 to explain. The Star Wars droid "speaks" using only bleeps and whistles. But the audience relates to it perfectly well because the people use their imaginations to interpret what R2 is saying.

"Robots are much more comfortable to deal with if we keep their underlying, alien style of intelligence submerged," Wright said.

Wright's got two projects to test these theories out. The first is the series of films he's developing with Winter, the one starring robots.

The next, set to begin in the spring, is more ambitious. Wright will put radio-controlled robots in public situations -- waiting tables, selling clothes in a mall -- that force people to interact with the machines.

Wright's team will be gauging how people react to these robot encounters. He hopes they'll be as positive as the response to Wright's computer games.
