The color interface as we have internally codified the wavelength frequencies of light is certainly worthy of further curiosity.

The ancestral parents house, now a rental, is a one story hip roof bungalow. A plain Jane except for the somewhat strong medium green, with carbon black trim. The sunshade is around back, and the more I'm around it, viewing it from the jobsite at the adjoining house the more it recedes, assuming almost invisibility, another thing to ponder.

Is it fair to say we have represented the two presence of absence of light with black and white? Something or nothing, the most profound relationship possible?
Certainly they are not only extremes of a gradient on the spectrum like deep, deep purple darkness, and on the other end hot, red hot, then white hot?

Or maybe both?

And why only these colors, why not some we cannot presently imagine. Painters sometimes travel to the South of France or New Mexico, for the quality of the light, is this a clue that we are not seeing some hidden qualities in our local colors?

Nature signals with the black/white bands, it's true, generally advising caution, or warning. The police car shows the human recognition that it works. Way beyond this colors have a wide and subtle range of meaning in nature and culture, how did that evolve?
Color perception and implication is so internalized it becomes difficult to explain and impossible to look at with detachment, and the same goes for form.
This is one of Alexander's major obstacles, I think, in writing a "constitution" for beauty.
The sharp, spare, 90 degree top corner of the sunshade jars and disturbs, up close,alone, but from a distance with the whole house in the picture, a nicely rounded corner would jump out as incongrous extra care.
The perceptions and interpretations change depending on proximity and context. This consideration is in addition the matters of time, taste, style, refinement, ego involement, and quality you guys have already loaded on this poor little framework previously.
Maybe the interest has endured here because we can all be a bit superior and condescending about the whole matter so we comment from positions of strength.
I know that always gives me enough rope to hang myself.
Do something pagan, It's mayday, Dirk

First casual response to inquiry,trying to establish and ascetain from both sides whether styles and standards will mesh nicely in the context of goal descriptions and initial attempts at description of work to be done style, standards and level of finish etc. I would characterize this phase as, safe, polite, respectful, mutual pshycoanalysis and world view appraisal.
Sometimes things end here in an easy understanding way, from one side or the other or both.
If some level of trust, confidence, comfort, and "sympatico" is established here the next level usually consists of me refering previous work in the form invitation to view, if thats ok or there is previous familiarity, I make a call to estimate the job and do a consultation. I do not charge for this and the is still no pressure on either side to committ untill we wrap up this phase. At that time I give an open quote, either time and material or bid, ballpark figures which I try to make high enough to allow for the inevitable surprises or upgrades.

I work by drawing and dreaming(awake) until I have ideas that satisfy the requirements as interestingly(beautifully, elegantly, surprisingly, inventively, delightfully) as possible. Then I try to figure out how to manipulate the materials to get them to do what I have drawn. To Rob and Matt
You can see an interesting critique of computers as a design tool by Milton Glaser at http://www.icograda.org/web/feature-past-single.shtml?pfl=feature-single-2.param&op2.rf1=56

Glaser believes that digital design suffers from what he calls a "lack of fuzzyness" which tends to resolve ambiguities too quickly. He feels that there is a dialectic process of ideas back and forth between the designer and the drawing within the fuzzy period that is helpful in arriving at a really good idea, and that computers interfere with this. Something to bear in mind while designing virtual-reality design tools so that you can invent a solution to this matter.

Dirk, about the motivation to build a custom dwelling from scratch:
Tadao Ando, a self-taught Pritzker-prize-winning architect, was asked what motivated him to do architecture, and he replied "Hatred." He was so dissatisfied with the built world that he was moved to build something else.
I feel a somewhat related motivation, part frustration at what exists, and part pleasure of making things that satisfy and explore my own sense of rightness.

Regarding building inspectors and issues of power, and control of safety, I can offer two stories. One is about Frank Lloyd Wright. When he was working on the Johnson and Johnson Building, his design had some golf-tee-shaped columns that each needed to carry, say, ten tons of load. The inspectors disputed his design and demanded a full-scale test. So a column was built and the test began, but FLW stood right under it as he waved the crane to begin loading the column! When the load reached ten tons, he kept waving for more weight until it approached twenty tons. Each time more weight was put on he tapped the column with his cane, sensing it. Finally he stepped away, but had it loaded more until it collapsed.
Gaudi had a run-in with inspectors when he had huge columns like elephant's feet protruding onto the sidewalk. As he was adament that there were no straight lines in nature he refused to be dictated to by the sidewalk line, and he just built it over all official protest. Those geniuses...ya just gotta love 'em!

Matt Titus 03:29:35 4/23/2004
Hello all!

I'm a friend of Dirk's. I'm interested in architecture, but I work as a software engineer. Thanks to Silicon Valley bubble-era title inflation, I once had business cards that claimed me to be a 'product architect', and though this was clearly an example of an employer pandering to the egos of their engineers, I can see that there are many parallels between software design and architecture.

Rob, I really like your site. It's good to see someone using new tools in useful ways.

Someone mentioned _The_Production_Of_Houses_ the other day, and I recalled the difficulties Alexander encountered with the building inspector and city planner/architect people on that project. The building inspector et. al. wanted to see complete 'architectural' drawings of the houses, and objected to the materials and construction techniques that Alexander was using. He seemed pretty upset that they were giving him such a hard time. One thing in particular that stuck with me was his complaint about the drawings. He was doing some of the design of the houses as he went, and said that it would bankrupt the project if he had to have detailed plans drawn for each unique house, and have the plans redrawn each time he modified the house. A while back Dirk observed that he used two ways of creating things - a cut-and-fit approach for some things, and a plastic arts approach for things that could be molded or sculpted. One of the really remarkable things about Alexander's approach was that he blended the two - he was almost sculpting the houses as he went, and used cut-and-fit only where necessary. That made the planners and inspectors uncomfortable, as they were used to buildings being completely planned before ground was broken. The other problem they had was with Alexander's unconventional construction materials and techniques, both of which (incidentally) were very much plastic arts - light concrete vaults supported initially by lathwork and canvas, and building blocks cast out of local soil.

I got to wondering how these sort of problems with the establishment could be overcome without incurring the exhorbitant cost of many changes to drawn plans. First, assume that the building establishment is not going to change, or will only change slowly and reluctantly, so someone who wants to build must accomodate them. Computer modelling of the houses and site could help; certainly there's nothing more plastic than bits. Cost would be kept to a minimum if the modelling software could produce plans that were acceptable to building inspectors and their ilk, though one would have to be careful how often one called the inspector to validate a new set of plans!

The problem with unconventional building materials and techniques, would be solved if the materials and techniques became conventional. Structural features could be designed by 'real architects' and validated by engineers. So if you want a light concrete vault above a room, pick and place it into your virtual plan. The software will tell you if the distance you are trying to span with the vault is too great. When you have completed a part of the plan, have the program produce a 3D model of it from any viewpoint you like.

While this is a far cry from the sculpted houses CA made in Mexico, tools like these could put the power to design buildings into the hands of ordinary folks, which would be a welcome change from having to consume an identical house in a vast bland tract. If you've made it this far, well, I surely value your comments!

Ciao,
