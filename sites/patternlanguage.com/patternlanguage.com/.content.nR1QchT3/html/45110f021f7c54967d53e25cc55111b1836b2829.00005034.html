PRINCIPLES FOR FAIRVIEW

The following sections should help us all understand the essence of our evolving master plan, and the essence of the community we hope for, for Fairview.

SOME BASELINE IDEAS
  • Overall gross density of six houses per acre with on 200 acres,with 100 acres let open natural or agricultura. and 100 acres contains 12 houses per acre net.
  • How far from house to natural land: no more than 100 feet.
  • No house lots: but zones for establishing the building able area and the garden.
  • Walled gardens.
  • Typical target range of houses ranging from $120,000 to $210,000 for raw house construction. This does not include infrastructure (global) and infrastructure (local), nor fees for house design and planning.
  • Each family-group (about ten families) get to design the local street, parking, commons together and lay it out together on the land.
  • Each family gets to design their home.
PATRICK'S ECOLOGICAL RULES
  1. CLOSE THE CYCLE: Develop integrated and on-site energy, waste, water and food production systems: - Generation of renewable power using hydrogen fuel cells - On-site composting and recycling center in co-ordination with Marion County's Solid Waste Management and Recycling Program - On-site gray and black water treatment facilities - Urban agriculture and community gardens EXCELLENT. BUT consideration might be given to local sand filter beds with lower costs, and more integrated with agriculture in the fields immediately next door to houses)
  2. BUILD WITH ZERO IMPACT TO THE REGIONAL WATERSHED: Incorporate Pringle Creek and existing wetland projects into protected riparian habitat and passive recreation areas, with a minimum development setback of 100 feet from each side of stream. EXCELLENT
  3. DESIGN A PERMEABLE URBAN FABRIC: Design built areas to have 90% effective permeable area. Excellent idea but of course only possible to achieve 90% of 70% of the land, since at the designated densities 30% of the land will have building footprints (presumably non-permeable)
  4. RESPECT EXISTING LANDSCAPE: Respect existing trees and topography to retain sense of place and park area, which should comprise 50 acres of the site. Definitely good as far as it goes but it should be enlarged to include agriculture and otherwise harvestable natural land, and should be held to comprise at least 100 acres of the site, possibly 130-150.
  5. RE-USE AND RETROFIT: Renovate as many of the 60 existing buildings as possible and recycle materials from unused structures. "Many" is good, and clear: "As many as possible" is a very strong statement and maybe clear , but I dont know the full reason for it.
  6. BUILD GREEN: Utilize the U.S. Green Building Council's LEED standards in the renovation of existing buildings and creation of new ones. OK .
  7. BUILD AFFORDABLY: Allow benefits of cost effective green building and infrastructure techniques to make 1200 dwelling units planned for the site more affordable. Needs to be spelled out why green building will reduce cost. It will reduce running cost, but not clear it will reduce capital cost.
  8. BUILD DIVERSITY: Provide a diversity of housing types for mixed age and income levels and a variety of tenure types, ranging from private to communal. Needs discussion of specifics to see if people are comfortable. Slight danger it might be like social planning!
  9. INNOVATE ARCHITECTURAL FORM: Allow the site, new green building technologies and community life to contribute to the innovation of architectural form. This could possibly be re-written so it does not become a wilful architectural trip. I would prefer to remove it
  10. CREATE A CENTRE: Concentrate community activities at the 'crescent'. Include one day care center, one elementary school and youth centered facilities such as working educational gardens and nature areas. OK
  11. AMENITIES AND TRANSIT CLOSE AT HAND: Dramatically reduce automobile use on the site by providing amenities and a full range of transportation modes within a 5-minute walking distance of all residences. Not entirely sure what the second part means : it could be laudable, but it might not be
  12. DESIGN WALKABLE NEIGHBOURHOODS: Design pedestrian friendly public realms that encourage walking and biking.I imagine most people will agree to this
  13. DESIGN GREEN MOVEMENT WAYS: Build narrow, interconnected and low impact street/path systems which are at least self mitigating and at most contribute to the ecological health of the site. Yes
  14. GENERATE LOCAL EMPLOYMENT: Foster a center for commerce, service-oriented business and zero impact industry. Absolutely yes to the need to have commerce and employment on the site. But there is no clear rational for them being grouped in a single area. Could even be harmful by restricting private enterprise
COMMENTARY ON ECOLOGICAL RULES (as written September 16)
    In general terms these principles seem attractive and positive. However, as presently stated they contain a core of very big assumptions, not entirely happy in my mind -- because they almost seem to veer too closely to perpetuate the 20th century suburban dream in slightly surprising form.

    The biggest of these issues are:

  1. The industrial "park": Separation of work from home. This harks back to the 1970's. I believe the community should consciously encourage work in the home or in attached workshops and offices which are parts of the home, thus reintegrating what the 20th century tore asunder: work and family life. Only very few kinds of light industry need to be rigorously separated.
  2. There is (at present) a similar separation of park from agriculture, and of houses from agriculture. I have been imagining a far more profoundly integrated weave of houses, gardens, fields, productive fields, meadows for pleasure etc -- but all interwoven with the houses -- not kept separate "over there" in a park. As stated so far, the conception of the park has overtones (unintended, I am sure) of an almost disneylike voyeur quality, that nature is to be looked at at: but through a plate glass window. In my conception, people would be using fields, growing things, working with animals, working outdoors, making productive use of the land around their houses and a little further from their houses.
  3. And consequently, I would expect to see a finer tissue of interaction between open land and houses/gardens, which gives every single household IMMEDIATE access to the park land and to the agricultural land, continuing the productive relationship which is the real meaning of "oikology", and which will allow people to be happier, more integrated, and communal not merely when they meet in the coffee shop, but in the process of what they are doing together, in streets, paths, fields, and flower beds and orchards.
    TO ACHIEVE THIS INTEGRATION: WE NEED INTERACTION OF HOUSES AND WORK AND LAND
    • I would state the rule as "Each house is within 100 feet of open land, not more" (open land being defined as a swath of nature or agriculture, at least a few hundred feet wide)
    • The settlement is laid out so that there is a very long perimeter for narrow ribbon-like compact groups of buildings and gardens, always lying "IN" the land, with open land on either side.
    • Above all, one senses, from every house, and immediate connection to nature.
    • Given the fact that houses will be smallish, and lots also smallish (because of density), the appeal of the place will come from the fact that each person feels that the fields, orchards, gardens, belong to everytone, and belong to each household. This can only happen if people really have all that stuff right outside their own front door; hence the 100 foot rule.
    From our conversation Russ, I am not sure that you subscribe to this description, and it may be the most delicate issue to have come up so far. I do not want to state my opinion too strongly, until I have seen the land.
    CARS AND ROADS
    • The way the plan works, should, I believe, allow people normal use of their cars.
    • However, it is to be laid out so that most movement within the site will be on foot or bike.
    • There are no parking lots as such: cars are jammed in among things, with as little space as possible given to the cars.
    • Very little asphalt.
    • Pedestrian paths have dominance in all cluster layout.
    • It is possible to drive from one house to another, but it will be a circuitous route, which does not invite this kind of use.
    • Some cars are gathered together in barns, not so convenient to the houses.

    All this is fully consistent with Patrick's statements about cars.

    Infrastructure (networks of sewer, water, electricity, drainage
    • The overall philiosophy of infrastructure is to put in as little as possible up front, and to use an approach which allows each network to be built incrementally, when needed, not all at once at the beginning.
