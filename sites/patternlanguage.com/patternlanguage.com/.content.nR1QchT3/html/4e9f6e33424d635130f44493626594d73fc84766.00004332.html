Urban profit
Win-win for renters, developer at 91-unit Berkeley high-rise

   Sunday, July 22, 2001

Most real estate developers will tell you with a sad shake of their heads that it's too expensive and too political to build quality apartments in urban areas unless they're for well-to-do renters.

Just don't drop that excuse anywhere near the doorstep of Patrick Kennedy, who's nearing completion of a 91-unit apartment building in the heart of downtown Berkeley.

Not only has he done it, but the Gaia Building apartments were designed to exploit the richness of the urban lifestyle - with some Parisian-style Left Bank entertainment pizzazz thrown in for good measure.

Kennedy's seven-story complex is less than a block from a BART subway station and the University of California at Berkeley campus, and steps from gourmet dining and nightlife.

Residents need venture no farther than a short elevator ride to the building's ground floor to catch theater performances, lectures, poetry readings, seminars and other cultural activities that are open to the public as well.

Everything is so close to the apartments that most residents don't own cars.

If they occasionally need wheels, renters can pick up a car from the building's auto-sharing pool. That arrangement may be a first for any apartment complex in the country. And of course, the cars are electric.

Wheelchair users can maneuver easily in the handicap-accessible apartments that exceed requirements of the federal Americans With Disabilities Act. Each unit has multiple telephone lines and high-speed T1 Internet connections in the living room and bedroom.

Affordable rents

The Gaia's best feature may be that renters don't need the income of Julia Roberts or Shaquille O'Neal. One-fifth of the apartments have been set aside for disabled, homeless, Section 8 and other low-income families - double what Kennedy was required to include.

Their apartments are no different from those of the college students and young professionals who pay market rents that start at $1,200 for one-bedroom apartments and $1,900 for two-bedroom units.

That's on a par with the $1,214 average rent paid in the Oakland area for all apartments - including older units without Gaia's amenities - during the first quarter of this year, according to a new survey by MPF Research, a consulting firm in Dallas.

It's significantly below the first-quarter average of $2,523 paid in San Francisco's South of Market neighborhood of luxury live-work lofts and upscale apartments.

The Gaia Building is Kennedy's version of urban utopia. It pleases him no end as he stands on the seventh-floor rooftop garden, wearing a white hard hat as workers apply the finishing touches, and surveys spectacular views that circle from UC's iconic Campanile to the Golden Gate Bridge.

The $11 million complex is 70 percent preleased. He expects it will be completely rented by the time the dingy, gray covering around the building is pulled back for the Aug. 1 opening.

"I happen to think that you can do well and do good," Kennedy said of the Mediterranean Revivalist-style midrise structure, the largest building constructed in downtown Berkeley since World War II. "I'd like all my projects to add something beyond mere physical space.

"Cities are important for the transmission of values and ideas and the sheer joy of living," he said. "I love cities and have been advocating for more than a decade that Berkeley adopt a more European model for its downtown commercial corridor."

Kennedy, 47, has developed more than 200 housing units - all in Berkeley - since founding Panoramic Interests in 1989. He has a master's degree in real estate development from MIT and graduated from Harvard Law School, but launched his real estate career after concluding that law was not his calling.

He worked for five years as a licensed contractor - he built his own home - and for two years as a BART real estate analyst.

His first entrepreneurial deal came after he moved in with his older brother, who was living in a six-unit apartment building designed by Julia Morgan.

The brothers eventually bought the building from their landlord. The younger Kennedy was on his way, building small-scale lofts, townhouse and apartment projects.

Building on beliefs

His last three ventures served to refine his philosophy of marrying the arts and nearly car-free living in quality apartments that poor and working- class families can afford.

The pleasure that Kennedy gets from building in an intense urban environment - and being able to make a profit at it - isn't enough to attract many of the country's for-profit real estate developers, according to a recent survey by the National Multi Housing Council.

Private apartment developers blame high land costs, fierce opposition from NIMBYs (not in my back yard) and zoning restrictions for not meeting the increasing demand for moderate-priced apartments.

Two-thirds of the developers in the survey said building such apartments made economic sense only in locations away from downtown urban centers where land is less expensive.

Kennedy circumvented those stumbling blocks with a tried-and-true two-step. Rather than buy the land outright, Kennedy often uses options to obtain the land. The maneuver allows him to tie up the property but not actually buy it until later, reducing expensive mortgage and other carrying expenses.

As he did in the Gaia deal, he also frequently includes the landowner as a partner, another move that holds down carrying costs.

"We have found many landowners in Berkeley who have underutilized property which they'd like to develop, but lack the capacity or the wherewithal or the inclination to do the development," Kennedy said. "So we'll do it with them."

Kennedy said patience and persistence are his defense against NIMBY's. "We are not afraid to spend four years in the development process," he said.

"Very few developers have the stomach or the patience for protracted battles. We know the stuff we're doing is not only going to help the area, but will be successful."

City's concession

There was also a bonus from the city of Berkeley. The city allowed the developer to tack on two additional floors of residential space in exchange for providing a cultural center, including a 99-seat theater, to showcase local talent.

Originally, the building was going to be home of the Gaia Bookstore, where poet Allen Ginsberg made his last public appearance before his death. The bookstore fell on hard times and folded 21Ú2 years after the apartment project was approved.

"Now it's the Gaia Bookstore without the bookstore," said Kennedy.

In Greek mythology, Gaia was the feminine spirit of the universe.

Already scheduled are 24 weeks of theater with the Shotgun Players, a decade-old guerrilla theater ensemble that will perform classic and contemporary plays on a black box stage across from a cafe that will feature poetry readings, recitals and other artistic programs.

Swing dancing to come

"We'll bring in some swing dancing," Kennedy said. "Berkeley is in sore need of swing in my opinion. The question is, is Berkeley ready for swing?"

Kennedy was able to carve a big chunk of the cultural center space from the garage area - thanks to the building's pedestrian-friendly location and proximity to transit. Some of the garage space was freed up with the installation of an innovative, German triple-stack parking system that stacks 39 cars in 13 spaces.

Most real estate entrepreneurs experience a bit of angst as a project nears completion. Kennedy is no exception. Twenty-four feet of richly colored tile crowns the building, but he is not sure how the facade will look once the protective canvas is removed.

"I just hope people don't think that the building looks like a Moorish bathhouse," he said.
