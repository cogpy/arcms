Crooked Little Man 23:22:41 11/24/2003
I enjoy the abstract ruminations, but I'm too busy most of the time. Here are a few more hot tips from my kit box for you hands on architect types.
Crown moulding is a beautiful thing, the upside down and backwards part adds mystique, as does the $500 compound mitre saw, but there are a couple of other ways:
1) The hand coping saw along a line scribed by holding a dummy piece cut at the magic 31.6 degree angle.
2) The protracter saw guide adjustable square set at 31.6 to give your hand held power saw set on its own 31.6 bevel the straight and true compound angle you're after.

Big,generous base boards are another thing out of fashion now that go a long way toward a comfortable room.
Don't overlook the possibility of the oversize half-round spline under the trim lintel as a cheap way to add a subtle touch of distinction to door and window casements, alternatively the plain or embossed corner square also kicks some high-end bandicoot, for next to nothing.
A quick thought that may help on tile floor layouts: Create your pattern like a carpet leaving a nice plain "field tile" border. You can do all your fudging and uneveness here and it won't show especially if you use larger stuff. What can really make this trick work and boost your design's power is to lay in an intermediate border between your design and the field, takes the heat off both sides and really looks like you're dialed into the ancient masters! Speaking of baseboards, a tile one is the only sensible one in a wet place like the bathroom, While you're at it, reward yourself, plug up your tub overflow outlet and see what a full bath can really be, freedom can happen in small ways too, remember?

A crooked little man 22:35:15 11/18/2003
I like to build it my way!
An eight penny nail works as well as a twist drill for predrilling, then use the phillips bit for a quick countersink.
A four inch disc sander is just about like a router for rounding over or chamfering boards.
Trim boards with generous reveals are more forgiving and add strong interest.
Mitered corners always open up at the heel used outdoors. A plain 90 degree joint will stay tight for years.
Saw for clear wood and use short lengths butted to longitudinals on 2',3',or 4' centers for siding, wainscoating or ceilings, ask the Japanese if you don't believe me.
An outdoor bathhouse is best, attatched or free standing, otherwise put an exterior door in the bathroom accessing a private deck or small porch.
The kitchen is the active heart of the home, give it visual access/authority over important parts of interior living and important outdoor space.
A dutch-style split back or side door is a very good thing, if you are brave enough to try it, such a door in the right circumstance can transform the interior and exterior spaces it serves and can bestow great peace and satisfaction with one's house.
A skylight usually works better than anticipated
Houses that are comepletely encicled by a nice walkway are quite rare, but generally very pleasant to spend time in and around.
The most valuable thing about many great houses, expensive and cheap, is how they relate to their surroundings, from within and without.
Linseed or Tung oil cut with turpentine is an excellent treatment for exterior wood furniture, fences, decks, arbors, and siding, and it can be pigmented for some great effects you will never find at the store.
When you stretch a design element past the conventional limit it will generally continue to work--but, it will require supporting suroundings to make changes to fit the new effect, to re-establish a new harmony.
Wood is nice ,White is nice, and so is color. Mix in some stone, tile, and brick. Pay a lot of attention to the light and sight lines and a good house begins to appear. Maybe built perfectly level and straight, maybe not, The more you design and build, the better you and your designs will get, that's just how it works!
