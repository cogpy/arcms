You said you have no carpentry skills. I'm wondering if you read the section of the patterns that show how to construct thick walls using cement and forms. It doesn't take much skill, just an attitude to do it. Ken Kern wrote a book about building your own house and another about making a masonry stove. I bet you sew. That qualifies as at least some experience in my book. And I bet you cook. You can follow a plan, use tools to complete the task, measure, cut, and divide materials, and appraise quality throughout the task. Don't let a cultural bias toward women building hold you back.

Jill,

Hunting for real estate can be a pain. So much of what appeals doesn't show on the listing data. (I chuckle trying to imagine some of the patterns being printed on the listing, like: positive outdoor space; half hidden garden; cascade of roofs.) Then you have to ponder the lending rate. Will it rise tomorrow? Is it better to jump on something with potential, or wait for something better??

My purchase was almost accidental. My mother has owned a 10 acre property near Westcliffe CO for 30 years. We had tried to find it 3 times through the years and failed. I was never excited because I thought it was just scrub and sage and boring. We went on a Friday 2 years ago and visited the tax assessor and he copied a map highlighting the path. It is a beautiful property. Lots of pinon pine, a huge red cliff mountain to the west, and no neighbors visible from the land. I was excited and my mother said I could have it to build on. The next day she said I would have to share it with my sisters. I've always wanted to build a house but could not imagine how such a project could be shared. So I thought the best thing to do would be to buy my sisters out. I started looking on the internet for comparable properties in the immediate area. Thats how I found the property I ended up buying. It had a house that burned down, a good garage (24x36), well, septic, electric, telephone and a driveway. Electricity was more than a mile away on my mother's land. Telephone is up to her property line, but everything else would cost. I figured the price I paid was less than the cost of the same improvements on my mother's land.

I have spent the 18 months since I purchased the land just cleaning up. I bought a small tractor and have moved all the debris out of the old basement. I did add some radiant foil insulation in the garage because I have been "camping" in there when I am on the property.

Because I am obsessed with building and feel like I may never finish at this rate, I am closing my business in Denver, selling my house, and moving up to the mountain. I am in a hurry because I want to get it on the market before the interest rate shoots up.

I am an eclectic reader. I have read a few books about bees and making mead, and a chess book, but most of my reading has been related to building. In addition to the pattern language, I have read a couple books about solar designs, ferrocement water tanks, stonework (lots of stone on my property), and thinshell concrete habitats. I was fortunate to meet George Nez last year and helped him build a demonstration thin shell concrete roof for the University of Colorado. I struck up a friendship with him and have done more of these roofs including a demonstration on my own in Oregon this year.

I am pleased that we have started a dialog here. I hope it encourages others to join in. I had hoped a practitioner would moderate and guide the discussion. Nevertheless, just the process of putting thoughts down in writing and getting feedback will be very beneficial. I hope it is for you as well.

I have worked on the problem posed in my first post. I stayed several days on my property with the patterns close at hand. I reviewed many patterns.

In pattern 110-Main Entrance, it says "Place the main entrance of the building at a point where it can be seen immediately from the main avenues of approach and give it bold, visible shape which stands out in front of the building."

Since my driveway wraps around the building site, it is possible to approach the house from either East or North. I must make the main entrance visible from both approaches. The North approach will provide a good guest parking area, a close-to-the-building parking spot for me and be closest to the main entry on the NE corner. The Eastern approach suggests a nice stairway from the lower level to the NE corner. I think this will be the best location for the entry.

Now I have another problem to pose:

What should I do with the existing basement?

Here's some background detail;

The fire left a 27'x36' cinder block basement with an East facing walkout on the same level as the garage 100' away.
At least the top 3 courses of cinderblock are fire damaged.

The South edge of the basement is only 2'from the well head and only 6-8' from the electrical service pole on the SW corner. The exit to the septic system is in SW corner of the basement.

If I utilize the existing foundation, I may need to replace all of the cinder block.

Using the existing footprint, I cannot build an overhang on the South side of the house because well problems would require pump truck access.

The foundation is dug into rock - not cheap.

Here's some possibilities:

-repair the cinder block and the existing footprint.

-lay plastic pipe for a geothermal heating system, then fill in the basement.

-build columns on the existing grade beam to support trusses and a large (40x40) platform to add design flexibility.

-create a hidden space in the basement along the South edge of the existing basement, thus moving the south edge of the "house" above away from the well head.
