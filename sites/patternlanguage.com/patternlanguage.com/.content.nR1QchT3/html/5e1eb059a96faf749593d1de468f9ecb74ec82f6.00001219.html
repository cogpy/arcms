Gordon Petrequin 12:40:25 5/20/2010
To: Margaret Magnus From Amherst (and whoever else is interested)
From Gordon Petrequin (California, Belgium and France)

You said, "then the entire plantation is also a whole, with each tree in the plantation being its own whole within the greater whole." ... My gosh, from 2007. Has it been that long since I visited this site (Pattern Language Assn)?

What you describe above is perhaps one of the most important characteristics of life. Let me put LIFE in caps, because it is the biological process that was born out of the interplay between matter and energy on this planet. This process brought life forth from simple molecules who nehaved according to their inner properties. We call this process, Evolution. Well, when we study its history, we see how it produced larger and larger organisms that were more and more diverse and became more and more complex and finally more and more intelligent. This is the historical trajectory of this process.

Now there is another important property of the life process. It is the thing you referred to. It is that as organisms evolved, they also joined together for the benefit of each one. In the fight for survival, organisms join other organisms to form a super-organisms. Due to the greater inner complexity and vitality achieved by these super-organisms, they achieved a greater probability of survival than would be the case if each sub-organism tried to survive on their own. This pattern is seen throughout the plant and animal kingdoms from the microscopic cells, bodily organs and organ systems, to the anthills and beehives, to the flocks of birds and herds of animals, to the primate families, tribes, races, religions, nations and perhaps, someday, a single planetary ecosystem.

Whether or not that arrives, there is a basic principle that surviving systems follow. When individuals enter a larger social system, in response for the nourishment and protection they receive they must in some way contribute back to the community of which they are a part. If they do not contribute, they become a parasite and reduce the survivability of the total community. This is why it is so important when we design homes and neighborhoods and towns we give a high priority to spaces that facilitate its members being able to help one another. As an elderly person, I feel proud of every contribution I am able to make to my family's prosperity, health and quality of life. I also contribute to the future of our neighborhood, town and country. It is not just an obligation. It makes me feel better and more alive. I am thankful that I can contribute. I wish there were more opportunities to contribute.

Ciao - Gordon Petrequin

Feynman says that anything fundamental says something about everything, and I nominate this series for one of the truly great works of the 20th Century. I'm overjoyed that it exists, and I apply the thoughts to every area of my life.

I'm a linguist studying a field commonly called sound symbolism, which takes issue with Saussure's 'Arbitrariness of the Sign'. This arbitrariness is the analog of the arbitrariness that arises in architecture as a result of a purely mechanistic world view. Language cannot in principle function without an 'arbitrary' (i.e. unrelated to form) denotation, and a non-arbitrary (i.e. synaesthetic) connotation.

But because of the times we live in, in linguistics too, the sign is decreed to be arbitrary, and poetry to be an impossibility.

Ronald Gombach 09:39:31 1/18/2007
A decade ago the re-reading of parts of "Timeless
Way" and "Pattern Language" influenced my choice
of domain name (back when you actually still had
some choice in domain names) and concept for a
project I wanted to implement in the form of a
website.

Along the way I got sidetracked by thinking and
experimenting too much, and what resulted was a
hodge-podge sandbox of experiments whose focus
changed as frequently as my personal whims and
other misdirected influences.

Recently, I began the journey of reading The Nature
of Order, and subsequently, the universe has been
hounding me with silent messages something like
"shame on you, get back to your original focus and
goal."

So here's where I'm at. I am trying to decide
whether to concentrate my time on bootstraping a
project that would begin to identify, catalogue, and
share "places that are alive" with context -- what's
near, history, images, etc. What I call (to myself) the
encyclopedia of places that are alive ... but with a
particular goal. I am (for this project) not interested
in cathedrals or public or institutional spaces/
places as a focus, rather a resource for people who
are, like myself, somewhat location-independent,
empty-nest-looking-to-downsize, attracted to
adaptive reuse live/work spaces ... churches that
became residences ... I think often of the paper mill
converted to condos in New Hope PA, overlooking
the Delaware ... a place that is truly "alive" but not
within my economic comfort zone, and not in an
area of sufficient diversity for our personal taste.

Places which are "alive" that are peripheral to these
"living places" are of interest as part of the context I
want to implement and manage (hopefully, along
the way others will be inspired to share and
contribute). But the goal is first and foremost to
create a virtual space where people can browse and
discover, and, ultimately, forced them rather than
be "glued to the computer," push back their chair,
get outside, across town or region or country, and
"look around" for that place which makes them
alive.

A favorite line of mine from Tracy Kidder's "Home
Town" reads: Sooner or later the suspicion that
someplace else is better, disturbs the peace of
home." A considerable number of location-
independent, thinking-independent pre-boomers
(myself included) and rushing-in boomers form de
facto class of people whose peace-of-home is
disturbed, and are looking to relocate across town
or across the state or across the country -- it
matters not ... if they only knew to where. You can
go to the Internet and "search" for a home ... but in
a highly constrained way, in ways that presume you
have already narrowed your geography to some
specific zip code or school district. I for one want a
place I can browse and discover "patterns" of place
and context. Not as an exercise in itself, but
something that might inspire me to go have-a-look
for myself.

Were I as economically independent as I am
location independent, I might just spend my time
fully wandering and browsing in search of my
"place." But I'm not.

Greatly appreciated are any comments,
suggestions, criticisms or directional-pointing to
resources that might help further such an
endeavor. Given the influence CA's writing has had
on my aspirations, this seemed a good place to put
forth a brief account of my "story." I look forward to
any "pattern" that might reveal itself as a result.

So far, in reading (I'm half way through Book I) I am
enjoying a paradox ... while I struggle a bit with
parts, I find it "comfortable" at the same time. It's
an interesting emotion ... for me, while reading, I
get a feeling that reminds me of listening to Keith
Jarrett's "Koln" and "Sun Bear" solo piano concerts. I
guess I have to label it as the feeling "W".

Ronald Gombach From Morrisville PA, United States
