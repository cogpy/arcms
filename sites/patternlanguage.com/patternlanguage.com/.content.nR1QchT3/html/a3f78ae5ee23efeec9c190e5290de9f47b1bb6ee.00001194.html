Monumental Chinese architecture was traditionally laid out according to cosmological principles that became known as feng shui- away of arranging objects to avoid or deflect the invasion of evil forces.
the Daoist Buddhist and Confucian temples you see in China today are a product of these principles.
Protected by rectangular wallslike a small city the temples main entrance faces south.
Inside the massive wooden doors is a series of pavillions and couryards, lined on both sides by residential and administrative halls. The inner complex can be compact or vast, consisting of hundreds of buildings or a small handful.
The temple architecture is virtually identical regardless of the religion practiced within. Variations in style are a product of region and historical period, not the dictates of faith.
The main support consists of interlocking wooden beams and enormous columns resting on raised platforms. The curving roof is paved in ceramic tiles. In southern China elaborate ornamentation and increasingly upturned eaves became the fashion. In the north temple architecture remained more restrained.

Chris
Yes, only a little in Australia. Lots of information around about it but not much experience using it.
My limited experience of it is that its labour intensive, particularly the rendering part. The bales can go up quickly but you have to watch out for lateral stability, as well as detailing around doors and windows. There is also the issue of load bearing or infill... load bearing is difficult, bales need to have great consistency in compactness, moisture content, particular straw should be used etc...
The general consensus down here seems to be it is particularly good for:
Rural situations where the bales are availabe and dont have to be transported long distances
Good for extremely cold and extremely hot climates
The bales have infinte insulation and no thermal mass, therefore around here they are often used as external walls
Not good for:
Damp climates
Urban buildings as the bales take up too much space.
I think they require more specialisation and experience than is thought.
Since the introduction of straw bale down here, there have been other 'straw' based waste products also, such as straw board panels. These are used as an internal cladding, much easier and faster to use.
Very early on I talked about my experience of how bad 'patterns' are so persistent in our culture.
I have had some more recent experiences and was wondering what others do about them.
The particular pattern I am talking about is 'Backyards'.
Just recently I completed a house design with clients, fairly young, in which we used many of CA's patterns... starting off with the major ones of siting, locating gardens in the best, sunniest positions, working through circulation realms and main room positions etc... The product was a great design. We also used some patterns that we invented ourselves, such as 'south facing outdoors', which is shade.
One day, the clients came to me after talking to friends and family, showing off their design we had made, and were told by people that they had no 'backyard'. The pattern of the backyard, like many other patterns, is so consistently repeated and deeply ingrained and entrehched as a pattern, that it pulls extremely hard, away from what I understand this 'unfolding process' to be, or away from people to do naturally what they should. Christopher Loyd 12:50:59 2/01/2004
I've tried for the first time in years to apply CA's ideas to design, in an architectural setting. I won't go into details (e-mail me if you have any questions), but here's what I've learned:

1) CA is known, though seen as kooky in contemporary education. I use "kooky" as a catch-all term, because everyone has differing opinions of the man, though it's easy enough to notice that openingly talking about CA is like talking about Bible. People either go really quiet, or a fight erupts.

2) When using APL, use the methodoly according to the book. Focus on one pattern at a time, in a given language sequence. I selected no more than ten patterns, and have applied three.

3) Don't mention patterns by name. Do talk about the desired trait or result. You want arcades because you want a transitional space between the outside and the inside, or whatever. Sell your idea in whatever way you can to get the design that is the best.

4) Keep it simple. Don't go onto the next pattern until you have fulfilled the first one. Build small, simple models, adding detail as time goes on.

5) Go with the flow. If the instructor suggests something different, go ahead and incorporate it, unless it completely contradicts what you're trying to do. For me, I've gone along with his suggesting, APL-izing it the whole time.

6) Don't make enemies. If anyone asks you why you're doing what you're doing, explain simply that people like XYZ, and that you want to accomplish that, hence your design. If you format what you're saying into something objective ("People like views"), it forces the other person to consider your statement as FACT, not OPINION. If they disagree, inquire into the facts. By keeping the discussion factual, you can only argue by what is known, not believed. Conversations usually don't go far. The other person loses interest. Who wants to talk about the study that showed that x-amount of people preferred 'Y'?

I haven't presented my project to a public audience. That will happen tomorrow. I will post the results of that.

Anyway, back to how great architecture is done:

In February 1921, Morgan wrote to Hearst about an idea for constructing some steps at San Simeon so the guests would have an memorable view of the main building:
While standing at the point (A) yesterday and looking up imagining the towers, the fine "looming up" effect grew on me-and the regret that few would see it often from this point. Then it occured to me, why not... continue the steps on down...a strikingly noble and "saississant" effect would be impressed upon everyone on arrival.

Hearst noted in the margin:

Heartily approve those steps. I certainly want that saississant effect. I don't know what it is but I think we ought to have at least one such on the premises.

(Saississant is a French word meaning "gripping".)

That architect-client relationship endured for another 25 years, a continuation of the highly succesful work Morgan had already done for Hearst's mother Phoebe, in the preceding two decades.
