The home we currently live in has some lovely qualities. We enter from a large entrance deck at the west (back) side of the house. My husband said as soon as he saw the quality of light in those two spaces, he knew it would be a good place.

The home we're planning to build is a monolithic dome home. Have any PL people been involved with the construction of a home like this? I am most certainly not expecting free design advice - I was hoping to make contact with some people here who may have actually worked on a similar project.

The question of time is interesting when it comes to life.

If you are talking about real communities, they take hundreds of years to organically grow.

It's not a huge step to take the clients brief, or their needs, into a pattern language for the project... most of the design information is, including the solutions and the spaces, within the clients own brief, or their expressed needs.

Money?
It probably does not boil down to an idividual viewpoint of money, but it probably is more related to culture and class.
Those who are more willing to spend money on improving their life, generally have more money to spend, not only on design but across many areas of their lives...
Those who are least willing to spend money on design, generally are also those who have least money to spend. l believe that although this is less true in western countries, it is more true on a world scale. It is also true in newly developing western countries.

In the example l gave of my experience with migrant working class clients... it is true that most now could afford to pay for design but they don't for many reasons. Two that stand out for me are:
1. Design does not give them the 'bang for their buck', where mental labour is undervalued in comparison to physical labour. They would pay you to dig a trench but not to design their home.
2. Most migrant working class clients come from peasant villages where they built their own homes... this was so for as far as they could remember... their were no architects.

People who come from post industrial societies, anglo's, as clients fall into two categories, when it comes to paying for design.
1. Those who can't afford design
2. Those who can afford it but can't build. The 'pattern languages' have been lost. They were lost through the industrial process.

Look around in your community and see who pays for design and who doesn't. l think you will find that it is not just individual pespctive, but there will be some common patterns. l would be interested to hear your observations.

Hi Bella,

In response to some of your questions and comments;

1. l have not seen 'hard' evidence by others of PL reducing costs.
My understanding so far, of PL, is that the method and application of PL improves the meaning and increases the 'density' of built spaces... because of the way the patterns overlap, and because PL are based on needs rather than on current fashion.
From this angle, it can be claimed that PL do save costs.
This claim however is made by every other architectural firm... that architecure and architects, through expertise of design can yield optimum levels of usable space for construction costs. In reality, this is often not the case.

PL do provide a common language... particularyl if the brief for the project can be developed into a pattern language for the project.

The time savings of PL are that time 'wasted' in design is reduced because the PL are allowing 're-use' of experience and ideas, rather than re-inventing the wheel.
As a friend of mine once put it "architects, too much of the time are trying to invent a new roof (as and example), where, every possible roof that can be built, has been built".

From responses by clients, it seems that clients are pleased with the particular approach that PL use, as well as the built product that they end up with.
What is particularly revealing is the amount of clients who come unsatisfied with previous designs of architects and then are really pleased after re-designing their home by using PL.

2. l have some of my own personal ideas about why l think PL are not in more widespread use, but l do not know for a fact... or from any long experience.
Clients are more interested in PL than directors of architectural firms as they have more interest in their homes being designed to suit theur own needs.
Architects and directors are less interested as the profession tries to mystify architecture... a bit like keeping a secret recipe a secret, so that it can be sold.
Because PL are based on simple rules, there is a rejection on the grounds that they do not allow individual creativity. This is a serious misunderstanding about the role of rules in PL.
l have found that the rules infact allow greater creativity, as well as incredible flexibility in design. Clients can also relate to this, as well as the fact that PL rules talk about real problems and propose real solutions.

l think that PL do have a feminine quality. l do not know whether women are more 'predisposed' to PL because of this.
Many of the families l have worked with in design, the women have taken leading responsiblity and involvement in the design process and often the construction stage too.
The emphasis is in the connectivity of the patterns, particularly the connectivity of patterns dealing with external (garden) spaces and internal patterns of the building dealing with circulation.

The question of why patterns are not in more widespread use needs also to be answered together with other related questions about architecture and building.

The example of Henley Homes using bad patterns rather than good patterns is one example of this. It probably is cheaper for Henley to use foil rather than warm by the sun. The context that they do this in is big and deep.
As a starting point, town planning and the layout of streets and subdivisions ignore most things that would make the implementation of good patterns possible.
Town planning is in turn influenced by other things ie: economics and industry, politics and culture. As town planning design is not based on a wholistic approach itself, ultimately the problem becomes one of bad patterns begetting bad patterns.
