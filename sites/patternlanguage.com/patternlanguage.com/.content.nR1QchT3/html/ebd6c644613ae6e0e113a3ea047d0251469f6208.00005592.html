WELCOME TO URBAN PLANNERS AND CONGRESS FOR NEW URBANISM MEMBERS

Anyone who is part of the growing movement to revitalize and re-form the cities we live in, inevitably has the following concerns. There is a concern with legal tools and instruments to help a given neighborhood; there is concern with the geometry of the neighborhood; there is an abiding concern of the relation between pedestrians and cars, possibly one of the least well-solved aspects of the modern era; there is a concern with style, geometry and homogeneity of architecture within a neighborhood, and tools which might create physical coherence, without imposing a doll-house similarity on buildings; and there must be, of course, abiding concern for real life, that is, for the emergence of vibrant social and commercial life in the neighborhood.

And there is, of course, the huge question of development. How, why, and whether the development process is necessarily the main channel of re-generation, or whether there might not be other, equally practical ways of doing things, which are less vulnerable to the negative aspects of the developer's inevitable constraints and motivations.
