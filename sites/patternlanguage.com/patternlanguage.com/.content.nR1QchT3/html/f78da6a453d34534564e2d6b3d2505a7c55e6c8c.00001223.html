Jeffrey Empfield 10:05:49 5/04/2006
Pete, Steve,

You have many interesting strands going here.

I can't compete with you two in terms of antiquity but I can throw some weight around when it comes to moderation; I've built a 360sq.ft. straw bale cottage and a 720sq.ft. timberframe/straw cabin. But I want to hear more about the French house that started as a prehistoric cave dwelling! That sounds wonderful.

I like Steve's idea for a point system that would free people to build organically as prescribed in these books. I would advocate simplifying even further so that a few prerequisite principles, if followed, would suffice. Building incrementally, over time, with local (or at least simple) materials, and with input for redesign from people who use the place (and so people must use the place), and finally having the work done by aesthetically informed designer/builders who are in dialogue with their community is what's essential. TPL would be an invaluable perennial tool for these builders but I would guard against it becoming canonized or codified. I think they suggest this in TPL--that these are a preliminary set of descriptions. I often think of additional patterns that are not covered by the original 253 entries. The patterns that resulted in Steve's Puymeras house flowed through the people who built it, not in bureaucracy. I like the scale of the hut (or cave dwelling?) for many reasons including the freedom from building codes.

Your point re TPL being an early hyperlinked text is interesting. I think it is a an attempt to apply a scientific matrix to these organic, humanistic, artistic patterns that developed over all pre-industrial time. It was written for a scientific, computer generation and reflects this in its structure. It is a compelling attempt to validate timeless wisdom with contemporary systematic discovery and organization. Like science, it has power in its reductionism--"these are the pieces of beauty"--and in that process risks losing sight of the whole.

The Timeless Way of Building and parts of the Nature of Order are poetry, and incantation, and meditation on the whole. I think in both, CA mimics his approach to building with his prose: layering, repeating, stacking, "gradual thickening." Perhaps Timeless is an early, intentionally vague attempt to suggest the "Quality that has no name" and the Nature of Order is a later, more refined attempt to not only name it but to describe its taxonomy. The Patterns are tangible results of a process related to this Quality. By going directly to the patterns, we take shortcuts to functional shelter and landscape--the accumulated knowledge of our forbears.

When I first read Timeless I was struck by how it reminded me of Buddhism and Taoism. "The timeless way..." Perhaps the ultimate distillation of the quality CA is getting at is mindfulness. If we merely approach our controllable surroundings with mindfulness then perhaps we will embody the timeless way of building with our improvements. Having resources such as a copy of TPL on hand often helps. The hard part is that to be mindful, one must be engaged, and our modern lives squeeze out time with our landscape, garden, patio, and craft. Circumstances team up on us even when we're committed.

I enjoy reading how Pete and Maryellen used a pattern to establish their garden wall. Of course it also sounds like the area has been changing ever since they moved there and this is the organic progression of how they live in that place.

Did you fill in the pool? How did the rose garden come to replace the pool?

I like how this topic (beauty) turns people's prose to poetry: "And finally, this very spring, they needed the strong Boundary to differentiate the two centers in order for each to go deeper into the states to which they attain."

I would like to offer a position for the superiority--or at the very least, the value--of the hand-drawn plan. Like maps, plans drawn by hand offer many advantages. They familiarize one in a unique and needful way with the details and nuances of a structure. They highlight important features that only the human artist can recognize--they communicate better information. Human to human communication is retained in the hand-drawn map. It is less so with computer-generated plans and maps.

I've worked creating maps using GIS and I'm impressed with the power and utility of the technology. I am also aware of its limitations in terms of being a digitized representation several generations removed from the original landscape. You end up with extreme precision for calculation and analysis of a facsimile of questionable accuracy. People are seduced by the data and the images and forget that they're looking at something other than the landscape.

Anyway, hand-drawn plans may just be an advantage when you go talk to the officials. Take your copy of TPL. Tell them that in order to maintain the essence of your very old place, you have to pursue these pre-industrial arts. The medium will be the message.

I've been working on a map of my place in Pennsylvania for years. It's in its 3rd. edition now and I hope to update and refine it this spring. Compass, paces, 100' tape, pencil, paper, eraser. I'm adding individual trees and boulders now. I tend to note information during the mild months and work on the map in the winter.

Pete Small 12:25:04 5/01/2006
The Potting Table and The Rose Garden

Here in New Jersey, spring is all over the place. It is a great year for lilac and dogwood. Maryellen and I spent the weekend erecting a wooden board fence to separate our rose garden from our potting table and tool storage area. The result of this (173) Garden Wall is an electrifying increase in the profundity of both the chaotic industriousness of the garden work area and the serene formality of the rose garden. Separating these two very different garden functions is allowing each to be altogether alive without detriment to the other.

But why did it take us so many years to recognize that the simple, easy to build wall would be so effective? It is because the two areas began their lives as one outdoor, inground pool area. Gradually, the area of the yard near the shallow end of the pool became a potting table/garden work area and the pool itself became a rose garden. The potting table area held all the tools and supplies needed to nurture the rose garden and all our other garden areas. The rose garden's formality challenged the potting table area to be more organized. As time went on and the two areas matured they no longer required the direct interaction to maintain their states. And finally, this very spring, they needed the strong Boundary to differentiate the two centers in order for each to go deeper into the states to which they attain.

Jeff 13:44:43 3/03/2006
Pete,

After looking at this pattern (28 eccentric nucleus) again, I have a couple of comments and questions for you.

First, I think it's interesting that the pattern receives one asterisk in the authors' system signifying that:

"...we have made some progress towards identifying such an invariant; but that with careful work it will certainly be possible to improve on the solution. In these cases, we believe it would be wise for you to treat the pattern with a certain amount of disrespect--and that you seek out variants of the solution which we have given, since there are almost certainly possible ranges of solutions which are not covered by what we have written."

I have often pointed to this system of qualified certainty when responding to critics of APL who find it too absolute in language and attitude.

Second, this idea is based on research done in post-WWII London and other examples of older cities that haven't been formed exclusively in response to the automobile. I think the majority of American cities today exhibit the exact opposite pattern of business centers drawing "catch basins" away from the city center toward the periphery. They also draw on large suburban and rural catch basins that overlap more due to branding and consumer subcultures where people travel greater distances to get just the item, price, or atmosphere they seek.

Unfortunately, many urban centers are wastelands today. In other cases, they are being rejuvenated as "cultural districts" without serving the organic role of daily commerce and community. In rare cases they are being redeveloped as authentic centers under the rubric of new urbanism. Of course there are a few examples of cities that have retained the classic pattern described by "eccentric nucleus" with neighborhood markets, newsstands, book stores, and cafes that draw residents toward the city center.

In the best cities with which I am familiar, traditional centers are sustained by herculean efforts to keep shopping malls and big box stores at bay. Even in these cases, though, my own experience points to a more refined circulation pattern in urban areas based on specific, singular destinations (food co-ops, favorite restaurants, etc) and progressive infrastructure such as pedestrian zones and bike paths. If I can walk or bike someplace pleasant I'll go there even if it's further from home.

My questions are: why this pattern? Are you in the position to influence the planning of your community? Are you considering developing a business that would be influenced by this pattern? Finally, tell us more about your house and how/which patterns from APL have helped?

Thanks.

Jill,

One of my favorite things about Alexander is how he democratizes "architecture" and points out the superiority of intuitive building--the timeless vernacular. I think that you (and many other people) can design and build your own shelter, especially since you're familiar with this website and the associated books.

I am a designer/builder and although I grew up with a carpenter father, most of what I've accomplished I learned through reading and doing.

Here are a few suggestions:
1. Plan to build incrementally. Start with a small portion of your overall design and focus on developing your skills (both for design and actually building) on this project. Maybe this portion will eventually be an outbuilding, study, or shed, etc.

2. Have faith in your ability to learn, adapt, and "satisfice"--a term Stewart Brand uses in How Buildings Learn to describe the process of making things good enough. Over time, like evolution, satsificing gradually results in the timeless buildings and landscapes Alexander points to in APL. You can learn by reading, asking those who know, and by "just doing it." I learned how to bake bread from a book and then later how to cut timberframes and build with straw bales from books. I learned how to make and apply earth plasters through helping a friend who had learned from someone else. Plan to learn what you need to learn as you go. Alexander basically does this and describes his process in his many examples.

3. Since you're in an expensive building market, consider buying a timberframe or modular unit of some kind from a less expensive market in your region. This could get you started with a roof and basic structure that would be ideal for implementing Alexander's approach to on-site, experiential design for placing windows, doors, internal elements, landscaping, etc. It would also take some of the risk out of being a beginner builder since you wouldn't have to worry about structural safety so much.

I have lots of ideas along these lines; let me know if any of this is helpful.

Jeff
